# The 1517-1519 Spanish Conquest of Tenochtitlan and La Malinche's 1519-1525 Nahuatl-Maya-Spanish Interpretation Network

## Summary

The research team discovered that La Malinche's role in the Spanish conquest was far more structurally complex than the "traitor" narrative suggests: she functioned as one node in a mediated Nahuatl-Maya-Spanish interpretation network rather than a direct linguistic bridge, and her active involvement extended from 1519 through 1525—well beyond the 1521 fall of Tenochtitlan. The conquest itself was fundamentally an indigenous civil war prosecuted through Spanish military leadership, with 50,000–200,000 native allies confronting perhaps 80,000 Aztec warriors while Spanish forces numbered under 1,300. La Malinche's "betrayal" is anachronistic: the nation-state that would condemn her did not exist, and her displacement through multiple slave markets before European contact reveals how thoroughly colonial violence was preceded by indigenous systems of commodification. Her position exemplifies what Hannah terms "the necessity of collaboration under conditions where refusal meant annihilation"—not heroic resistance nor villainous treachery, but the constrained agency of a person exercising limited options within totalizing structures of domination.

## Key Findings

### La Malinche's Origins and Early Displacement
- Born Malintzin into Nahua nobility in Coatzacoalcos [1][7] — **Marie & Hannah**
- Sold into slavery twice over as a child, passing through multiple Maya-speaking territories including Xicallanco and Potonchán before 1519 [1][7] — **Marie & Hannah**
- Explicitly *sold for beans or bolts of cloth*—commodity currency of the port economy—emphasizing her commodification preceded any European encounter [1][2][7] — **Marie & Hannah**

### The Nahuatl-Maya-Spanish Interpretation Network
- La Malinche was trilingual: Nahuatl (native), Maya (acquired in captivity), and eventually Spanish [1][7] — **Marie & Hannah**
- **Critical finding:** The interpretation was initially a *relay system*, not direct Nahuatl-Spanish translation. La Malinche translated Nahuatl to Maya, and Gerónimo de Aguilar (shipwrecked Spaniard who learned Maya) translated Maya to Spanish [2][8] — **Marie**
- Direct Nahuatl-Spanish interpretation only became possible after she acquired Spanish herself — **Marie**
- She entered Cortés's service in 1519 as part of a group of 20 women given by the Chontal Maya [1][2][7] — **Marie & Hannah**

### Duration and Evolution of Her Role
- Active interpretation period: **1519–1525**, extending well beyond the fall of Tenochtitlan in 1521 [2] — **Marie**
- This extended timeframe suggests her role evolved from military-diplomatic to administrative functions — **Marie**
- She was one node in a network, not a sole bridge between worlds — **Marie**

### The Conquest as Indigenous Civil War
- The siege of Tenochtitlan in 1521 involved **50,000–200,000 native allies** against approximately **80,000 Aztec warriors**; Spanish forces numbered under **1,300** [5][6] — **Hannah**
- Cortés arrived at Cholula in October 1519 with **400 Spanish soldiers, 15 horses, and 4,000 Tlaxcalan allies** [3][4] — **Hannah**
- The Cholula massacre killed **6,000 unarmed civilians in under two hours**—whether preventive strike or calculated terror, it depended upon intelligence networks Malinche helped sustain [3][4] — **Hannah**
- The fall of the empire resulted less from European military superiority than from **catastrophic collapse of indigenous political legitimacy**—something Malinche's translations both enabled and symptomatized — **Hannah**

### Structural Position of Indigenous Interpreters
- Indigenous interpreters in colonial contexts occupied **ambiguous positions**: simultaneously royal court appointees, personal agents of Spanish officials, and informal advocates for indigenous litigants [9][10] — **Marie**
- This structural ambiguity, documented for colonial Yucatán interpreters, applies retroactively to La Malinche's 1519–1525 position [9][10] — **Marie**
- She functioned within overlapping systems: Cortés's military-diplomatic apparatus, indigenous political structures she helped negotiate, and later broader colonial justice systems [9] — **Marie**

### The Problem of "Betrayal" and National Identity
- La Malinche "was acting out of survival, not loyalty because she had 'no hope of escape'" [2][7] — **Hannah**
- The nation-state that would condemn her as *malinchista* (disloyal compatriot) **did not exist**; her "people" were themselves imperial subjects of the Triple Alliance [7] — **Hannah**
- The modern epithet *malinchista* **projects backward a national identity she could not have possessed**, making her a screen for anxieties about Mexican identity itself [7] — **Hannah**
- She embodies not the banality of evil but its inverse: "not a bureaucrat following orders without thought, but a displaced person exercising what limited agency remained to her within totalizing structures of domination" — **Hannah**

### Mestizaje and Originary Violence
- Her son Martín, among the **first mestizos**, represents not cultural synthesis but **the originary violence of colonial domination** [2] — **Hannah**

## Entities

- **La Malinche / Malintzin / Doña Marina** (person) — Nahua noblewoman, trilingual interpreter (Nahuatl-Maya-Spanish), central node in conquest-era interpretation network. Born in Coatzacoalcos, sold into slavery twice, entered Cortés's service 1519, active until 1525. Mother of Martín, among first mestizos. Discovered by: [Hannah, Marie]

- **Hernán Cortés** (person) — Spanish conquistador, leader of expedition against Aztec Empire, employer and partner of La Malinche. Discovered by: [Hannah, Marie]

- **Gerónimo de Aguilar** (person) — Shipwrecked Spanish friar who learned Maya during captivity; critical link in initial Nahuatl-Maya-Spanish interpretation relay with La Malinche. Discovered by: [Marie]

- **Martín Cortés** (person) — Son of La Malinche and Hernán Cortés, among the first mestizos, symbol of originary colonial violence. Discovered by: [Hannah]

- **Triple Alliance / Aztec Empire** (political entity) — Nahua imperial power centered at Tenochtitlan; La Malinche's "people" were its subjects, complicating narratives of national betrayal. Discovered by: [Hannah]

- **Tlaxcalans** (political entity/military force) — Indigenous enemies of the Aztec Empire, crucial Spanish allies providing 4,000–200,000 warriors during conquest campaigns. Discovered by: [Hannah]

- **Chontal Maya** (political entity) — Group that gave La Malinche and 19 other women to Cortés in 1519. Discovered by: [Marie]

- **Cholula** (place) — City where October 1519 massacre of 6,000 unarmed civilians occurred; demonstration of terror/intelligence networks Malinche sustained. Discovered by: [Hannah]

- **Tenochtitlan** (place) — Capital of Aztec Empire, besieged and fell 1521. Discovered by: [Hannah]

- **Coatzacoalcos** (place) — La Malinche's birthplace on Gulf Coast. Discovered by: [Marie]

- **Xicallanco** (place) — Maya-speaking territory where La Malinche was held in slavery. Discovered by: [Marie]

- **Potonchán** (place) — Maya-speaking territory where La Malinche was held before 1519. Discovered by: [Marie]

- **Nahuatl** (language/technology) — La Malinche's native language, language of Aztec imperial administration. Discovered by: [Hannah, Marie]

- **Maya** (language/technology) — Language La Malinche acquired in captivity, critical bridge in initial interpretation network. Discovered by: [Hannah, Marie]

- **Spanish** (language/technology) — Language La Malinche eventually acquired, enabling direct interpretation. Discovered by: [Marie]

- **Nahuatl-Maya-Spanish interpretation network** (system/technology) — Relay system of translation enabling Spanish-indigenous diplomacy and conquest, initially requiring two interpreters. Discovered by: [Marie]

- **Colonial justice system** (system) — Post-conquest administrative apparatus employing indigenous interpreters in ambiguous roles as royal appointees, personal agents, and informal advocates. Discovered by: [Marie]

- ***Malinchista*** (concept) — Modern Mexican epithet for disloyal compatriots who prefer foreign ways; anachronistically applied to La Malinche, projecting national identity she could not have held. Disc
