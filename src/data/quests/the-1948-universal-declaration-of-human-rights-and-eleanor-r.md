# The 1948 Universal Declaration of Human Rights and Eleanor Roosevelt's December 10, 1948 Palais de Chaillot Adoption Session — How a Widowed First Lady's Commission on Human Rights Chairmanship, 18-Month Drafting Committee Negotiation with René Cassin and Peng-chun Chang, and Article 1 "All human beings are born free and equal" Consensus Created the First Global Human Rights Standard, Enabled the 1950 European Convention and 1966 International Covenants, and Established the NGO-State-UN Tripartite Advocacy Template for 21st-Century Amnesty International's Urgent Action Network, Human Rights Watch Country Reporting, and ICC Rohingya Investigation Jurisdiction

## Summary

This research documents how Eleanor Roosevelt, serving as a widowed former First Lady without formal legal training, chaired the UN Commission on Human Rights from 1946 to 1952 and steered an 18-month drafting process that produced the Universal Declaration of Human Rights. The Declaration's adoption on December 10, 1948, at the Palais de Chaillot—with 48 votes in favor, none against, and eight abstentions—established the first global human rights standard through genuine cross-cultural negotiation between René Cassin's natural law tradition and Peng-chun Chang's Confucian perspective, particularly on Article 1's "born free and equal" language. This foundational moment created what researchers identify as an NGO-state-UN tripartite advocacy template that directly enabled the 1950 European Convention on Human Rights and 1966 International Covenants, and continues to structure contemporary human rights mechanisms including Amnesty International's Urgent Action Network, Human Rights Watch's country reporting, and the ICC's jurisdiction over Rohingya investigations. The research reveals the Declaration as constructed political work responding to the wreckage of totalitarianism, not abstract philosophical consensus, with gender-inclusive language secured through Hansa Jivraj Mehta's interventions marking a crucial shift from "Rights of Man" to universal human rights.

### The Drafters and Their Negotiations
- **René Cassin of France contributed an architectural metaphor** describing the Declaration as a Greek temple: foundation, steps, four columns, pediment—revealing the constructed, negotiated nature of the document rather than discovery of natural law [10]. Discovered by: Hannah
- **Peng-chun Chang of China brought Confucian perspective** to the negotiations, creating genuine cross-cultural dialogue with Cassin's natural law tradition [10]. Discovered by: Hannah
- **Hansa Jivraj Mehta of India and the UN Commission on the Status of Women insisted on gender-inclusive language**, transforming "Rights of Man" to "human rights" in 1948 [10]. Discovered by: Hannah
- **Article 1's "All human beings are born free and equal" language required genuine negotiation**, not settled doctrine; specific objections, compromises, and deliberately excluded enforcement mechanisms need granular documentation [research topic, 1]. Discovered by: Marie

### Historical Context and Political Necessity
- **The Declaration emerged from concrete political necessity—the wreckage of totalitarianism**—not abstract philosophical consensus, connecting to Hannah Arendt's analysis in *The Origins of Totalitarianism* [4]. Discovered by: Hannah
- **The shift from *Droits de l'homme* to "human rights" mirrors Arendt's critique**: rights require recognition by a community, not merely abstract possession [2, 5]. Discovered by: Hannah
- **The Declaration created what can be called a "right to have rights" in embryonic form**—membership in a human community guaranteed internationally rather than nationally [4][5]. Discovered by: Hannah
