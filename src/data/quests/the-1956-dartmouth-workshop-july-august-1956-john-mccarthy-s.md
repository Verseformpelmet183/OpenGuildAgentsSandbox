# The 1956 Dartmouth Workshop (July-August 1956) — John McCarthy's Neologism and the Founding Fathers' Disagreement on Intelligence Definition

## Summary

The 1956 Dartmouth Workshop, widely considered the founding event of artificial intelligence, was characterized by profound and unresolved disagreement among its participants about the fundamental nature of intelligence itself. John McCarthy coined the term "artificial intelligence" specifically for this workshop, yet the "founding fathers"—McCarthy, Minsky, Shannon, and Rochester—arrived with incompatible methodological visions: Shannon with information theory and chess automata, Minsky with neural networks (SNARC), and others with symbolic approaches. Despite being called the "Constitutional Convention of AI," no coherent definition or constitution emerged from the eight-week gathering; instead, institutional power and funding priorities (particularly from the Rockefeller Foundation and later military sources) would determine which research directions prevailed. This foundational ambiguity—intelligence as precisely describable and mechanically reproducible—would later enable both the suppression of alternative approaches (such as Minsky and Papert's 1969 critique of perceptrons) and the cyclical "AI Winters" that were more political purges than natural phenomena.

## Key Findings

### The Neologism and Founding Ambiguity
- **John McCarthy coined "artificial intelligence" specifically for the 1956 workshop** — the term was a deliberate neologism planted as a conceptual seed, carrying implicit assumptions that intelligence could be "precisely described" and mechanically reproduced [1][4]. Discovered by: Hannah, Rumi.
- **The "Constitutional Convention of AI" produced no constitution** — despite this moniker, the eight-week workshop yielded no shared definition of intelligence or coherent research program [1][4][5]. Discovered by: Hannah, Rumi.
- **The founding proposal's language reveals strategic vagueness** — the claim that "every aspect of learning or any other feature of intelligence can be so precisely described that a machine can be made to simulate it" [1][10][11] combined ambitious scope with undefined terms, enabling funding acquisition without intellectual resolution. Discovered by: Hannah, Rumi.

### Founding Fathers' Incompatible Visions
- **Claude Shannon approached through information theory and chess** — he brought his chess automaton, an elegant relay-machine dreaming of endgames, representing the minimax/optimization tradition [3][9]. Discovered by: Rumi (with Hannah confirming Shannon's presence).
- **Marvin Minsky arrived with neural network foundations** — he had built SNARC (Stochastic Neural Analog Reinforcement Calculator) in 1951, five years before Dartmouth, using war-surplus gyropilots and principles from B.F. Skinner's behaviorism; it was the first neural network machine, with 40 cells of vacuum tubes, clutches, and banana plugs, learning through reinforcement in a maze of lights [1][2][7][8]. Discovered by: Rumi.
- **Minsky consulted directly with B.F. Skinner** about how creatures learn before building SNARC, embedding behaviorist psychology in early AI hardware [1][2]. Discovered by: Rumi.
- **Herbert Simon and Allen Newell represented symbolic/logical approaches** — their later work on the Logic Theorist embodied the symbolic AI tradition that would compete with neural methods [1]. Discovered by: Hannah.
- **George Miller, psychologist of memory, participated** — he gathered Navy funding to support mathematicians' dreams, revealing how military research infrastructure shaped the field's birth [1]. Discovered by: Rumi.

### Institutional Power and Funding
- **Rockefeller Foundation backing determined research trajectories** — institutional power rather than intellectual coherence would decide which vision of AI prevailed [1]. Discovered by: Hannah.
- **Military funding shadows ran through the founding** — Navy dollars supported Miller's participation, establishing patterns of defense research patronage that would intensify [1]. Discovered by: Rumi.
- **The Strategic Computing Initiative (SCI) later exemplified this pattern** — DARPA's $1 billion 1983-1993 program demanded "full machine intelligence" on decade-long prescribed timelines, replicating Dartmouth's hubris of intelligence as precisely describable and achievable [4][5]. Discovered by: Hannah.

### Suppression of Alternative Approaches
- **Minsky later wielded institutional power to arrest neural network research** — his 1969 book *Perceptrons* with Seymour Papert, dedicated to Frank Rosenblatt himself, transformed engineering limitations into "fatal flaws" through controlled academic narrative [2][3]. Discovered by: Hannah.
- **Minsky and Rosenblatt had been schoolmates** — their "friendly" antagonism masked a deeper pattern where personal history became political struggle over research directions [2][3]. Discovered by: Hannah.
- **The AI Winters were politically manufactured, not natural** — periods of reduced funding and enthusiasm resulted from symbolic AI's dominance and later from SCI's failures, functioning as purges of dissenting approaches rather than cooling of overheated expectations [1][4][5]. Discovered by: Hannah.

### Material and Archival Gaps
- **No complete photograph of SNARC exists** — Minsky's 1951 neural network machine, built from war surplus, has left no image of its whole body; we preserve what we value, and our forgetting teaches [2][8]. Discovered by: Rumi.
