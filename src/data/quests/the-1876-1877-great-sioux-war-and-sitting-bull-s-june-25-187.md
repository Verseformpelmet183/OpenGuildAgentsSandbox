# The 1876-1877 Great Sioux War and Sitting Bull's June 25, 1876 Little Bighorn Victory — How a Hunkpapa Lakota Holy Man's Sun Dance Vision, 2,000-Warrior Alliance Coordination, and Custer Battalion Annihilation Created the Last Great Native American Military Victory, Accelerated the 1887 Dawes Act Assimilation Policy, and Established the Resistance-Through-Sovereignty Template for 21st-Century Standing Rock DAPL Protests, Tribal Casino Gaming Compacts, and Federal Recognition Jurisdictional Battles

## Summary

This research reveals that the 1876 Little Bighorn victory constituted a paradoxical turning point in Indigenous-American relations: Sitting Bull's spiritually-coordinated military triumph simultaneously demonstrated the effectiveness of pan-tribal sovereignty-based resistance and provoked the systematic federal policies designed to permanently dissolve such collective Indigenous power. The Sun Dance vision that unified 2,000 warriors across multiple nations created a template for coordinated resistance that the U.S. government spent the following century attempting to dismantle through allotment, blood quantum, and cultural genocide—yet this same template reemerged at Standing Rock in 2016 and persists in contemporary tribal jurisdictional battles. The researchers discovered that victory itself became the catalyst for accelerated assimilation policy, establishing a pattern where Indigenous military or political success triggers intensified structural countermeasures rather than negotiated settlement.

## Key Findings

### The Military and Spiritual Dimensions of the 1876 Victory

- **Sitting Bull's Sun Dance vision** preceded the battle, showing soldiers falling into camp "like grasshoppers"—a prophetic framework that enabled the coordination of approximately 2,000 warriors from multiple nations [Rumi]. This represented *wakan* (sacred power) made manifest through military coordination, establishing the holy man's spiritual authority as the unifying force behind tactical success [Rumi].

- **The 2,000-warrior alliance** represented a temporary confederation of the Seven Council Fires (Lakota, Dakota, Nakota divisions) plus Cheyenne and Arapaho allies, coordinated through traditional warrior societies rather than centralized command [Rumi]. This decentralized coordination model proved highly effective against Custer's divided battalion tactics [1, Kongzi, Rumi].

- **The Custer battalion annihilation** (five companies, approximately 210 men) marked the most significant Native American military victory against U.S. forces in the Plains Wars, occurring on June 25, 1876, during the national Centennial celebrations [1, Kongzi, Rumi].

- **The Lakota were intruding on Crow treaty lands** at the time of the battle, having been displaced from the Black Hills by American gold rush pressures [1, Kongzi]. This displacement-of-displaced pattern complicated the moral geometry of resistance, with Chief Blackfoot of the Crow seeking American military assistance against Lakota incursions as early as 1873 [1, Kongzi].

- **American "divide and rule" tactics** exploited inter-tribal conflicts, securing Crow and Arikara scouts and allies against the Lakota coalition—representing what Kongzi identified as the antithesis of virtuous governance [1, Kongzi].

### The Paradox of Victory: Accelerated Retaliation and Policy Response

- **The 1876-1877 punitive expeditions** followed immediately: General Crook's "starvation campaigns," the 1877 forced Black Hills seizure negotiated under threat of winter annihilation, and relentless military pressure that made the victory unsustainable [Rumi].

- **The 1887 Dawes Act** (General Allotment Act) accelerated specifically in response to Little Bighorn's demonstration of collective Indigenous military capacity, replacing treaty-based collective landholding with 160-acre individual allotments designed to dissolve tribal cohesion [Rumi].

- **Sitting Bull's 1881 surrender at Fort Buford** occurred under conditions of mass starvation and buffalo extinction—not military defeat per se, but ecological and economic collapse engineered through deliberate policy [Rumi].

- **The 1885-1890 Buffalo Bill Cody period** transformed Sitting Bull from military leader to performed symbol, what Gerald Vizenor terms "survivance"—resistance through persistent presence even in commodified humiliation [Rumi].

- **Sitting Bull's December 15, 1890 assassination** at Standing Rock by Indian police bullets (two weeks before Wounded Knee) specifically targeted the holy man's ongoing spiritual and political authority, not residual military threat [Rumi].

- **Blood quantum calculations, boarding schools, and religious suppression** were systematically deployed to dissolve the collective spiritual authority embodied in the Sun Dance itself—the organizational technology behind the 1876 victory [Rumi].

### The Resistance-Through-Sovereignty Template and Contemporary Legacies

- **The 2016 Standing Rock DAPL resistance** directly invoked the same geographical location (Standing Rock), spiritual framework (prayerful resistance), and pan-tribal coordination as the 1876 template—demonstrating the persistence of sovereignty-based resistance strategies across 140 years [Rumi].

- **Tribal casino gaming compacts** represent a contradictory legacy: using federally-imposed jurisdictional frameworks (the legal residue of allotment and termination policies) to generate economic resources for collective tribal benefit, thus repurposing the structural mechanisms of assimilation toward sovereignty [research topic, both researchers].

- **Federal recognition jurisdictional battles** continue the fundamental conflict established in 1876: the U.S. state's attempt to reduce Indigenous political existence to contractual and commercial relationships versus Indigenous assertions of inherent sovereignty predating and superseding federal frameworks [research topic, both researchers].

- **The "resistance-through-sovereignty" template** combines: (a) spiritual/visionary foundation, (b) pan-tribal coordination across jurisdictional boundaries, (c) strategic use of media and public sympathy, and (d) persistence through transformation of tactics—visible from 1876 through 2016 and beyond [synthesized from both researchers].
